The Beginning

This was my first Himalayan trek my first snow, my first flight completely unexpected and unplanned. Nothing in my life had prepared me for it, and yet somehow, everything had. I arrived two days early, which gave me time to breathe, to slow down, and to let the mountains begin their quiet work on me.

Rishikesh welcomed me the way it welcomes everyone who comes with an open heart with the sound of the Ganga, the smell of incense, and the feeling that something in you is about to change. Watching the Ganga Aarti for the first time was nothing short of divine. The flames, the chanting, the river carrying it all forward I just stood there, completely still.

Witnessing the sacred confluence of two rivers at Dev Prayag was surreal. Two rivers — two separate journeys — merging into one. I didn't know it then, but that's exactly what this trip was about to do to me.
Dev Prayag — where the Bhagirathi and Alaknanda meet, and the Ganga is born. Standing here, everything felt like a blessing.
The Ganga at dawn — the light that told me I was exactly where I needed to be

The next morning, I rented a bike and rode alone to the bungee jumping point — just me, the road, and the mountains watching. There's something about riding solo through Uttarakhand that strips you of every excuse you've ever given yourself.

Bungee jumping had been on my bucket list for years. I finally checked it off — jumping over the Ganga, the river roaring below me, the world momentarily upside down.

Some moments are too alive to photograph. Bungee jumping over the Ganga was one of them. When you fall like that — with everything below you and nothing holding you — you find out very quickly what you're made of.

Then, the journey to Kedarkantha began — filled with music, laughter, incredible places, and the most amazing people. The food was incredible, the roads were winding, and somewhere in the middle of it all, I stopped thinking and just started feeling.

Sankri — Where It All Begins
Base Camp  ·  6,400 ft  ·  The first step into the Himalayas
A tiny bloom on the trail — the mountains' way of saying hello

We set off from Sankri base camp, trekking through rocky paths lined with vibrant red rhododendrons. Dancing on the mountain trails, enjoying every breath of crisp air, made the journey feel less like effort and more like celebration.

For the first time, I experienced 0-degree and minus temperatures. And in those conditions — shivering, fingers cold, breath fogging in the air — seeing those sunsets was something else entirely. The sky would turn amber and rose over the snow-capped peaks, and I remember thinking: this is why people come here. This is why they keep coming back.

Red rhododendrons lining the trail to Juda Ka Talab — nature's welcome committee, in full bloom
Day 2 · Juda Ka Talab

My First Snow.
The Peace I Was Searching For.

As we made our way to Juda Ka Talab, I saw snow for the first time in my life. I want you to think about that for a second — the first time. There are some experiences that can only happen once, and this was mine.

The view of the mountains, the golden oak trees glowing in the sunset, fluffy dogs wandering around, the crisp mountain air, the peace of stargazing under a clear sky — it was magical. And the people I met made it even more fun, playing games and sharing stories. That night, I had my first experience sleeping in a sleeping bag. Freezing. Absolutely unforgettable.

I found a fallen oak tree, and I lay down on it for a while — completely at peace, free from every constraint that life had ever put on me. It was like I had been searching for this feeling, and I finally found it. Just for a moment. Just right there.

I thought the food would be bland at high altitude, but every meal felt earned and tasted better for it. The dogs that wandered through camp were our protectors, our companions — one in particular was so beautiful, so fluffy, that if I could have, I would have packed it in my bag and brought it home.

Juda Ka Talab — sitting still in the snow, the bare oak trees all around me, the silence so full it felt like company
02
Juda Ka Talab — The Dog That Found Me
9,100 ft  ·  First snow · Golden oaks · A trek buddy I'll never forget
The fluffiest soul on the mountain — I would have carried it home if I could

Climbing the snowy trail, I tried to throw snow at my friends — and hilariously slipped and fell instead. I lost count of how many times I fell that day. Every single time, I laughed harder than the time before. There's something about falling in the snow that makes you feel like a child again.

And then — this dog. My trek buddy. The most beautiful, fluffy mountain dog, walking right beside me like we had known each other for years. It was so pretty that I genuinely stopped mid-trail just to take it in. If I had a chance, I would have packed it up and brought it home without a second thought.

The mountains were showing me things. The lake at Juda Ka Talab — the way it sat there, completely still, surrounded by snow and pine and silence — was simply breathtaking. I stood at the edge of it and felt, without any doubt, that I truly belonged there.

Snow-capped peaks above Sankri — clouds wrapping the mountains like they were being kept secret. This view made every cold, every ache, worth it entirely.

Base Camp — Day 3

The Tent Zipper.
The First Snowfall.

We arrived at Kedarkantha base camp. And at that moment — standing there with snow-covered mountains stretching endlessly before me — I just stood still. Mesmerized. Feeling like I truly belonged to this place, like it had been waiting for me.

We were inside the tent, playing games, laughing, warm in that thin nylon shell against the cold. And then a voice from outside. Someone unzipped the tent — and there it was. Snowfall. Real, silent, beautiful snowfall.

I still smile like a child when I think about it. Snow touching the golden oak trees. Snowflakes landing on my face. We built a snowman. I fell in the snow so many times I stopped counting — and every single fall made me laugh harder than the last.

That night, the snow kept falling. The whole night, the tent filled under its weight, the path outside became invisible, the wind picked up. We waited. We hoped. The campsite had more prayers going up than I could count.

Base camp after the snowfall — colourful tents against endless white, mountains keeping their secrets in the clouds
A Night to Remember

Stargazing at
12,000 Feet

We lay under the Himalayan sky, learning constellations, speaking softly, then not at all. The cold didn't matter anymore. The ache in my legs didn't matter. Up there, under that sky, everything that had ever felt heavy simply dissolved.

Some experiences can't be photographed. Only lived.

Summit Day — The Mountain Decides

The Summit That
Wasn't Meant to Be.

Morning came. The snow had fallen all night. The path was gone — buried under white. The wind was still there. The conditions hadn't cleared. We waited for the signal that it was safe to go, that the mountain would let us pass.

It didn't come.

The summit attempt was called off. We stood there — all of us — looking up at Kedarkantha above us, hidden somewhere in the cloud and snowfall. And here's the thing: I wasn't crushed. Not even close. Because somewhere between Rishikesh and that snowy base camp, something had shifted in me.

What happens, happens for good reasons. The mountain calls you when you are ready. And it will call me again — I know it will. It reminded me, once more, that it is not the summit. It is never just the summit. It's the path. The journey. The people you meet along the way.

I couldn't complete the summit this time. But I left Kedarkantha with something I had been searching for far longer than any trek — a kind of peace that doesn't leave you when you come back down.

I'll be back, Kedarkantha. I'll be back.

Somewhere on the trail — back to the camera, face to the Himalayas. This is the version of me I came to find.
Summit night — all our boots in a circle, all our poles meeting in the middle. We were ready. The mountain wasn't.
