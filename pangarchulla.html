The Beginning

I had planned for Kuari Pass. That's what I had booked, what I had packed for, what I had told everyone back home. But somewhere between Rishikesh and the mountains, the plan dissolved — and something better took its place.

We started in Rishikesh. December Rishikesh. The kind of cold that makes you want to stay inside, and somehow also makes you want to jump into a river. We sat by the ghats that first evening and watched the Ganga Aarti — the flames, the chanting, the river carrying everything forward. There were hundreds of people around me and I barely noticed any of them.

So many people, so much noise — and somehow the quiet found me anyway. The Ganga does that. It just keeps going, and something about watching it makes all your noise go quiet too.

The next morning, rafting. December water. Cold as hell. The guide said jump in. I looked at the Ganga, took a long breath, and jumped. Freezing, euphoric, chilled and alive and laughing — all at the same time. And then getting out of the water in December with the wind on your wet clothes and all of us standing there laughing like it was the best thing that had ever happened. It kind of was.

Ganga Aarti, Rishikesh — flames on water and a feeling something in you is about to change
Mt Dronagiri at first light — huge, unhurried, entirely unbothered
Dec 21 — Karchi, Tugasi Village

Ten Hours on a Mountain Road.
Then Dronagiri.

Rishikesh to Karchi village near Joshimath is ten hours in a tempo traveller. Winding roads, music, the slow kind of conversation that only happens when you're going somewhere together. We arrived at night, dark and tired. Slept immediately.

Then morning came — and with it, Mt Dronagiri. Standing exactly where it had always stood, lit by the first sun, enormous and completely still. It asked for nothing. It announced nothing. It was simply there. That felt like enough.

01
Karchi → Gulling Campsite
Dec 22 · 2,875m  ·  Alpine glow on my vision board for a whole year. Finally.
The trail through small mountain homes — slow, humble, gorgeous

The trail started in a village. Small homes, narrow paths, tiny shops, and more dogs than I could count. Children jumping around with joy that doesn't need a reason. Women working in farms, children on their backs, utterly unbothered. The most grounded people I have ever met anywhere.

December meant no snow — dry sand and dried leaves underfoot instead. We reached Gulling campsite and I did what I always do: found a spot. My speciality, wherever I go. Not to do anything, just to sit. Just to be quiet with the mountain for a while.

Then the thing I had been waiting for the whole year happened. Alpine glow. I had it on my vision board — a photograph of mountain light going orange. And there it was, the sun falling on Dronagiri, the rocks going amber, the snow catching fire without burning. I sat there and could not look away. It feels like the mountains are smiling at you, sharing something they don't share often.

Alpine glow on Dronagiri — I had this on my vision board for a whole year. It was everything.
Gulling · Night One

Lying on a Mountain Rock
Under the Stars

After the alpine glow, the stars came out. I lay down on a mountain rock and looked up. I could point out constellations. I watched them moving — all in one direction, slow and certain. Temperature dropped to zero. My hands were freezing. I didn't move. The sky was too important.

Morning came and I sat opposite Dronagiri with my sketching pad and scribbled something. It didn't look like the mountain. I think that's fine.

Gulling campsite, 0°C, hands freezing — some experiences cannot be photographed, only lived
02
Gulling → Khullara + The Decision
Dec 23 · 3,378m  ·  Dense forest. The pine tree photo. Then the news.
The pine tree — the photos we took here became some of my all-time favourites

Day 2 was dense forest and steep climb. Streams crossing the trail. Rucksack getting heavier with every switchback. The fluffy trek dog walking beside me like we had always known each other.

There was a broken tree that looked exactly like the Iron Throne from Game of Thrones. I sat on it immediately. Then one pine tree caught my eye on the way through the forest, and we stopped — the photographs from that spot ended up being some of the best of the entire trip. Sometimes the trail gives you things you didn't plan for.

Then the news at Khullara camp. Kuari Pass had no snow this year. That was the buzz kill. But then — Pangarchulla had snow. Nobody had planned for Pangarchulla. It wasn't even in the consideration when I took the flight.

A crazy idea. The group looked at each other. We said yes. That's it. That's all it took.

That evening the alpine glow hit the Hathi-Ghoda peaks and Dronagiri from Khullara camp. Views in every direction. I sat there sipping soup, watching the mountains go dark, talking to new friends from Bangalore, laughing at nothing in particular.

Hathi-Ghoda peaks from Khullara — the mountains lit up like they were celebrating the decision we had just made
Midnight · My Birthday

A Cupcake at 3,378m.
A Wish. Then the Summit.

I woke up at midnight. December midnight at Khullara campsite, 3,378m, temperature well below zero. And then I realised — it was my birthday.

My friends had carried a cupcake all the way from Rishikesh. They lit the candle. I blew it out. Made a wish — which is a secret. And then we got ready to go climb a mountain we had not planned to climb.

I had wanted this for years — a birthday on a mountain. It happened. Not at the summit I planned for. At a different summit entirely, one I didn't know existed when I boarded that flight. I think that's the best kind of gift the universe gives you.

Summit Day — Day 3

One Step
at a Time.

We left at 1 AM. 26 people. 25 boys. One girl — me. The cold air hit my face like a wall and didn't stop. The incline started gentle and got less gentle very fast.

Midway, we hit Mini Pangarchulla — the checkpoint where the guide said if the group doesn't move fast enough, we all turn back from here. I already knew I wasn't turning back. I said it to myself, quietly: one step at a time. And then I just took those steps.

Departed 1:00 AM From Khullara basecamp, in the dark
Summit Reached 9:15 AM Pangarchulla Peak · 4,700m
Temperature −10°C Water bottle frozen solid in my hand
Made the Top 6 of 26 I was one of the six

Where the snow started, we put on microspikes. The water I had filled was frozen — I shook the bottle, broke it loose, sipped in small amounts, then kept walking. The headlamp was blinking. Cold air going through my lungs. My mind said: nope, you are not giving up.

The final stretch wasn't snow — it was black ice. 70–80 degrees steep. Rock climbing in places. There were sections where if you slipped even a little, you didn't just fall. I knew it. I climbed it anyway. My summit partner was a packet of Skittles. The sugar rush hit at exactly the right moment. I will never underestimate candy again.

The climb — headlamp, ice, cold air flooding the lungs, and one thought: one step at a time
Sunrise on the climb — I turned back and felt like the main character of the movie
The Summit · 9:15 AM

A Dog at
15,200 Feet.

As it was getting brighter I looked back — orange and purple sky, mountains in a row catching the first light, snow peaks going golden one after another. It looked animated. Too good to be real. I felt like the main character of a movie except this wasn't a movie, I was just living.

Then I reached the top. A tiny temple built for Lord Shiva. Red and orange prayer flags flying in the wind. And — I am still not over this — a dog. At 15,200 feet. Just sitting there, perfectly calm, completely unbothered, like it was the most normal place to be.

The trek lead knew it was my birthday. He handed me a Parle-G biscuit and said happy birthday. No gift will ever top that.

I turned in a full circle. Nanda Devi. Kamet. Hathi-Ghoda. Dronagiri. Trishul. Chaukamba Massif. 360 degrees of the Himalayas at sunrise, on my birthday. Out of 26, only 6 of us made it. I was one of them. I love myself for that.

The descent was the knees' problem. Sharp mountain air making sounds like someone whispering in your ear. Dehydration headache. Sun after hours of dark. At one point I slid down a stretch on purpose, hurt my bum a bit, and laughed. There will always be one person in the group with the crazy ideas. I am that person.

The 360° view — Nanda Devi, Kamet, Dronagiri, Trishul, Chaukamba. Everything all at once.
04
Kuari Pass + The Long Way Down
Dec 25 · 3,640m → Karchi  ·  Christmas on the mountain. We came this far — so we did Kuari too.

We had come all this way. So the next morning we woke up early and did Kuari Pass too — the trek we had originally planned. Then started the descent with full rucksacks. Heavy legs. Knee pain. That sharp mountain air making sounds in my ears like someone was there. People asked if they could carry my bag. I said no. Some things you carry yourself.

We made it back to Karchi just in time. And on the way back — a bunch of puppies on the trail. Tiny, warm, completely unbothered. The mountains give you a near-death experience on black ice and then immediately show you puppies. That feels very accurate to me.

Not all treasures are gold and silver, mate. For me, they’ve always been the mountains.

About the Author

Getting Lost

I'm someone who believes the mountains don't ask for your résumé — they ask for your presence. This blog is my honest, unfiltered story of the trails I walk, the fears I face, and the version of myself I discover every time I lace up my boots and head up.

Pangarchulla came out of nowhere. It wasn't on the plan. It showed up and asked if I was brave enough. I said yes before I had the sense to think about it. That is, I think, the only right way to answer a mountain.
